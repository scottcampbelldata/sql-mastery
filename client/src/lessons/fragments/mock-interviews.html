Mock Interviews

Four 45-minute simulations. These are not more practice problems - they are dress rehearsals. Set a timer, talk out loud the entire time as if an interviewer is watching, and grade yourself with the rubric afterward. Space them: one at the end of week 10, three across weeks 11-12. Every question runs against one of your two databases - chinook for clean business analytics, stackoverflow for the big-data rounds.

The rules

  • 45 minutes hard stop. Unfinished is data - it tells you where speed work is needed.
  • Narrate everything. Restate the question, name the pattern, state the grain of your output, announce your CTE plan, call out assumptions and edge cases. Silence is the number one mock-interview failure.
  • psql or your usual client, no notes, no earlier modules open. The curriculum will not be sitting next to you in the real thing.
  • Run your queries. Sanity-check row counts and eyeball a few rows. Interviewers watch whether you verify or just declare victory.
  • Solutions stay closed until the timer ends. Then grade every question, including ones you did not reach.

Grading rubric (score each mock out of 20)

Dimension0-12-34-5
CorrectnessWrong results, unnoticedMostly right, missed an edge case (ties, NULLs, fan-out)Right, edge cases handled or explicitly called out

Mock 1 - Chinook: "The new BI hire screen"

Straightforward business questions, escalating. This is what a first-round screen at most companies actually looks like: a small, clean sales database and a series of "tell me about the business" questions.

M1.1chinook5 min

How many invoices were issued in 2023, and what was the total invoiced amount that year?

solution
SELECT COUNT(*) AS invoices_2023,
       ROUND(SUM(total)::numeric, 2) AS total_2023
FROM invoice
WHERE invoice_date >= '2023-01-01' AND invoice_date < '2024-01-01';

Half-open range, narrated: "I use a half-open range so it is index-friendly and unambiguous about timestamps." The invoice data spans 2021-2025, so pick a year in the middle and say why.

M1.2chinook7 min

Revenue by genre, highest first, with each genre's share of total revenue. (Line revenue is unit_price * quantity from invoice_line.)

solution
SELECT g.name AS genre,
       ROUND(SUM(il.unit_price * il.quantity)::numeric, 0) AS revenue,
       ROUND(100.0 * SUM(il.unit_price * il.quantity)
             / SUM(SUM(il.unit_price * il.quantity)) OVER (), 1) AS pct
FROM invoice_line il
JOIN track t ON t.track_id = il.track_id
JOIN genre g ON g.genre_id = t.genre_id
GROUP BY g.name
ORDER BY revenue DESC;

Points earned for: reading the schema and using unit_price * quantity from the line table (say you checked there is no discount column - unlike some sales schemas), and percent-of-total via a window over the aggregate.

M1.3chinook8 min

Which customers have never been invoiced? And separately: which customers bought in 2022 but nothing in 2024?

solution
-- never invoiced: anti-join
SELECT c.customer_id, c.first_name || ' ' || c.last_name AS customer
FROM customer c
WHERE NOT EXISTS (SELECT 1 FROM invoice i WHERE i.customer_id = c.customer_id);

-- active 2022, gone by 2024: two EXISTS with opposite signs
SELECT c.customer_id, c.first_name || ' ' || c.last_name AS customer
FROM customer c
WHERE EXISTS (SELECT 1 FROM invoice i
              WHERE i.customer_id = c.customer_id
                AND i.invoice_date >= '2022-01-01' AND i.invoice_date < '2023-01-01')
  AND NOT EXISTS (SELECT 1 FROM invoice i
              WHERE i.customer_id = c.customer_id
                AND i.invoice_date >= '2024-01-01' AND i.invoice_date < '2025-01-01');

Narrate why NOT EXISTS over NOT IN (NULL footgun). The second query is churn analysis in disguise - say so. (In this small dataset the "never invoiced" set may be empty; an empty result is a correct answer, and saying "I would confirm with a COUNT" is the senior move.)

M1.4chinook10 min

Each support rep's top-selling genre by revenue: employee name, genre name, revenue. One row per rep. (Customers link to their rep via customer.support_rep_id.)

solution
WITH rep_genre AS (
    SELECT c.support_rep_id, g.name AS genre,
           SUM(il.unit_price * il.quantity) AS revenue
    FROM customer c
    JOIN invoice i      ON i.customer_id = c.customer_id
    JOIN invoice_line il ON il.invoice_id = i.invoice_id
    JOIN track t         ON t.track_id = il.track_id
    JOIN genre g         ON g.genre_id = t.genre_id
    GROUP BY c.support_rep_id, g.name
),
ranked AS (
    SELECT *,
           ROW_NUMBER() OVER (PARTITION BY support_rep_id
                              ORDER BY revenue DESC) AS rn
    FROM rep_genre
)
SELECT e.first_name || ' ' || e.last_name AS employee,
       r.genre,
       ROUND(r.revenue::numeric, 0) AS revenue
FROM ranked r
JOIN employee e ON e.employee_id = r.support_rep_id
WHERE r.rn = 1
ORDER BY revenue DESC;

Top-N per group in its most common costume. Name the pattern before typing; mention the ROW_NUMBER vs RANK tie decision.

M1.5chinook15 min

Monthly revenue for 2023 with: running YTD total, month-over-month percent change, and each month flagged 'above' or 'below' the 2023 monthly average. Present as one clean result.

solution
WITH monthly AS (
    SELECT date_trunc('month', invoice_date)::date AS month,
           SUM(total) AS revenue
    FROM invoice
    WHERE invoice_date >= '2023-01-01' AND invoice_date < '2024-01-01'
    GROUP BY 1
)
SELECT month,
       ROUND(revenue::numeric, 0) AS revenue,
       ROUND(SUM(revenue) OVER (ORDER BY month)::numeric, 0) AS ytd,
       ROUND(100.0 * (revenue - LAG(revenue) OVER (ORDER BY month))
             / NULLIF(LAG(revenue) OVER (ORDER BY month), 0), 1) AS mom_pct,
       CASE WHEN revenue > AVG(revenue) OVER () THEN 'above' ELSE 'below' END AS vs_avg
FROM monthly
ORDER BY month;

Three different windows over one aggregated CTE - running (ORDER BY), offset (LAG), and whole-set (empty OVER). If you wrote this inside 15 minutes while narrating, you passed the screen.

Mock 2 - Chinook: "The product analytics round"

Same database, harder questions. Now the interviewer wants ranking, two grains in one output, and a retention definition you have to defend out loud.

M2.1chinook5 min

Top 10 customers by lifetime spend: full name, country, total, invoice count.

solution
SELECT c.first_name || ' ' || c.last_name AS customer,
       c.country,
       ROUND(SUM(i.total)::numeric, 2) AS lifetime_total,
       COUNT(*) AS invoices
FROM customer c
JOIN invoice i ON i.customer_id = c.customer_id
GROUP BY c.customer_id, customer, c.country
ORDER BY lifetime_total DESC
LIMIT 10;
M2.2chinook8 min

Which artists have tracks in the most playlists? Top 10 artists by count of distinct playlists containing at least one of their tracks. (Chain: artist, album, track, playlist_track.)

solution
SELECT ar.name AS artist,
       COUNT(DISTINCT pt.playlist_id) AS playlists
FROM artist ar
JOIN album al          ON al.artist_id = ar.artist_id
JOIN track t           ON t.album_id = al.album_id
JOIN playlist_track pt ON pt.track_id = t.track_id
GROUP BY ar.name
ORDER BY playlists DESC, artist
LIMIT 10;

DISTINCT is the whole question: an artist with 50 tracks in one playlist counts once. Say that before typing - grain awareness is what is being tested.

M2.3chinook10 min

Quarterly revenue trend with quarter-over-quarter growth, and identify the single best and worst quarter by growth.

solution
WITH quarterly AS (
    SELECT date_trunc('quarter', invoice_date)::date AS qtr,
           SUM(total) AS revenue
    FROM invoice
    GROUP BY 1
),
growth AS (
    SELECT qtr, revenue,
           ROUND(100.0 * (revenue - LAG(revenue) OVER (ORDER BY qtr))
                 / NULLIF(LAG(revenue) OVER (ORDER BY qtr), 0), 1) AS qoq_pct
    FROM quarterly
)
SELECT * FROM growth ORDER BY qtr;
-- best and worst in one pass: rank the non-NULL growth rows from both ends
WITH quarterly AS (
    SELECT date_trunc('quarter', invoice_date)::date AS qtr,
           SUM(total) AS revenue
    FROM invoice
    GROUP BY 1
),
growth AS (
    SELECT qtr, revenue,
           ROUND(100.0 * (revenue - LAG(revenue) OVER (ORDER BY qtr))
                 / NULLIF(LAG(revenue) OVER (ORDER BY qtr), 0), 1) AS qoq_pct
    FROM quarterly
)
SELECT 'best'  AS label, qtr, qoq_pct FROM growth
WHERE qoq_pct IS NOT NULL ORDER BY qoq_pct DESC LIMIT 1;

Excluding the NULL first quarter from best/worst is the edge case. Partial first/last quarters are worth a spoken caveat.

M2.4chinook10 min

For each country with at least 5 customers: customer count, average lifetime value, and the name of the top spender. (Two grains in one output - plan before typing.)

solution
WITH ltv AS (
    SELECT c.customer_id, c.country,
           c.first_name || ' ' || c.last_name AS customer,
           SUM(i.total) AS lifetime
    FROM customer c
    JOIN invoice i ON i.customer_id = c.customer_id
    GROUP BY c.customer_id, c.country, customer
),
ranked AS (
    SELECT *,
           ROW_NUMBER() OVER (PARTITION BY country
                              ORDER BY lifetime DESC) AS rn
    FROM ltv
)
SELECT country,
       COUNT(*) AS customers,
       ROUND(AVG(lifetime)::numeric, 2) AS avg_ltv,
       MAX(customer) FILTER (WHERE rn = 1) AS top_spender
FROM ranked
GROUP BY country
HAVING COUNT(*) >= 5
ORDER BY avg_ltv DESC;

MAX(x) FILTER (WHERE rn = 1) smuggles the row-level winner into the group-level summary in one pass - the P7.10 trick reused. Recognizing reuse across problems is exactly what fluency is. (USA, Canada, France and Brazil clear the five-customer bar here.)

M2.5chinook12 min

Repeat-purchase behavior: what percentage of customers made a second purchase within 90 days of their first? Narrate your definition decisions.

solution
WITH ordered AS (
    SELECT customer_id, invoice_date,
           ROW_NUMBER() OVER (PARTITION BY customer_id
                              ORDER BY invoice_date, invoice_id) AS rn
    FROM invoice
),
firsts AS (
    SELECT customer_id, invoice_date AS first_purchase
    FROM ordered WHERE rn = 1
),
seconds AS (
    SELECT customer_id, invoice_date AS second_purchase
    FROM ordered WHERE rn = 2
)
SELECT COUNT(*) AS customers,
       COUNT(s.customer_id) AS made_second_purchase,
       COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE s.second_purchase <= f.first_purchase + INTERVAL '90 days')
           AS second_within_90d,
       ROUND(100.0 * COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE s.second_purchase <= f.first_purchase + INTERVAL '90 days')
             / COUNT(*), 1) AS pct_repeat_90d
FROM firsts f
LEFT JOIN seconds s ON s.customer_id = f.customer_id;

Decisions to say out loud: denominator = all customers with any purchase; "second purchase" = second distinct invoice; LEFT JOIN keeps one-time buyers in the denominator. Definition narration is most of the grade on retention questions.

Mock 3 - Stack Overflow: "The big-data round"

Same skills, but on tables where sloppy queries visibly hurt - a quarter-million posts, nearly a million votes. Runtime awareness is part of the grade here. Remember the schema: every column is lowercase with no underscores (owneruserid, posttypeid, creationdate, displayname), and the user primary key is id.

M3.1stackoverflow7 min

Posts per year, split into questions and answers as columns, with an answers-per-question ratio.

solution
SELECT EXTRACT(YEAR FROM creationdate)::int AS yr,
       COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE posttypeid = 1) AS questions,
       COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE posttypeid = 2) AS answers,
       ROUND(COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE posttypeid = 2)::numeric
             / NULLIF(COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE posttypeid = 1), 0), 2) AS ans_per_q
FROM posts
GROUP BY yr
ORDER BY yr;

EXTRACT in GROUP BY is fine here - no filter to keep sargable, and the whole table is being aggregated anyway. Saying that distinction earns the performance point.

M3.2stackoverflow10 min

Top 10 users by accepted answers: users whose answers were most often marked as the accepted answer on a question. (Requires the posts self-join on acceptedanswerid.)

solution
WITH accepted AS (
    SELECT a.owneruserid, COUNT(*) AS accepted_answers
    FROM posts q
    JOIN posts a ON a.id = q.acceptedanswerid
    WHERE q.posttypeid = 1
    GROUP BY a.owneruserid
)
SELECT u.displayname, ac.accepted_answers
FROM accepted ac
JOIN users u ON u.id = ac.owneruserid
ORDER BY ac.accepted_answers DESC
LIMIT 10;

Aggregate the big self-join down first, join to users last - the P8.7 lesson applied without being asked. NULL owneruserid rows (deleted users) drop out of the final join; mention it. Expect names like RolandoMySQLDBA and Aaron Bertrand at the top.

M3.3stackoverflow10 min

Reputation vs participation: bucket users into reputation deciles (NTILE), then per decile: user count, average posts per user, and percentage of users with zero posts.

solution
WITH post_counts AS (
    SELECT owneruserid, COUNT(*) AS posts
    FROM posts
    WHERE owneruserid IS NOT NULL
    GROUP BY owneruserid
),
bucketed AS (
    SELECT u.id,
           NTILE(10) OVER (ORDER BY u.reputation) AS decile,
           COALESCE(pc.posts, 0) AS posts
    FROM users u
    LEFT JOIN post_counts pc ON pc.owneruserid = u.id
)
SELECT decile,
       COUNT(*) AS users,
       ROUND(AVG(posts), 2) AS avg_posts,
       ROUND(100.0 * COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE posts = 0) / COUNT(*), 1) AS pct_lurkers
FROM bucketed
GROUP BY decile
ORDER BY decile;

The LEFT JOIN + COALESCE is what keeps zero-post users in the analysis at all - an inner join would silently define lurkers out of existence and corrupt every decile. About 184k of 248k users never posted, so the lower deciles are almost all lurkers. That one join decision is the question.

M3.4stackoverflow15 min

Answer-speed league: for questions created in 2018, the median minutes to first answer, by month. Then: which month was fastest, and what caveat applies to December?

solution
WITH firsts AS (
    SELECT parentid, MIN(creationdate) AS first_answer
    FROM posts
    WHERE posttypeid = 2
    GROUP BY parentid
)
SELECT date_trunc('month', q.creationdate)::date AS month,
       COUNT(*) AS answered_questions,
       ROUND((PERCENTILE_CONT(0.5) WITHIN GROUP (
           ORDER BY EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM f.first_answer - q.creationdate) / 60
       ))::numeric, 1) AS median_minutes
FROM posts q
JOIN firsts f ON f.parentid = q.id
WHERE q.posttypeid = 1
  AND q.creationdate >= '2018-01-01' AND q.creationdate < '2019-01-01'
GROUP BY 1
ORDER BY 1;

December caveat: right-censoring. Late-December questions have had less time to collect answers if the data snapshot ends near year-end - their unanswered ones are excluded and their answered ones skew fast. Naming censoring in a SQL interview is a differentiator few candidates reach.

Mock 4 - Stack Overflow: "The senior panel"

Hardest set. Ranking within groups, portfolio-health counting, a time-of-day heatmap, and a verbal data-modeling finale - all on the big database, where the analysis choices you make show up on a stopwatch.

M4.1stackoverflow10 min

Tag activity by year: for the five biggest technology tags (sql-server, mysql, postgresql, oracle, performance), the number of questions asked per year, and each tag's question-count rank within that year. Only rank 1-5 per year.

solution
WITH ty AS (
    SELECT CASE
             WHEN tags LIKE '%|sql-server|%'  THEN 'sql-server'
             WHEN tags LIKE '%|mysql|%'       THEN 'mysql'
             WHEN tags LIKE '%|postgresql|%'  THEN 'postgresql'
             WHEN tags LIKE '%|oracle|%'      THEN 'oracle'
             WHEN tags LIKE '%|performance|%' THEN 'performance'
           END AS tag,
           EXTRACT(YEAR FROM creationdate)::int AS yr,
           COUNT(*) AS questions
    FROM posts
    WHERE posttypeid = 1
      AND tags SIMILAR TO '%\|(sql-server|mysql|postgresql|oracle|performance)\|%'
    GROUP BY 1, 2
),
ranked AS (
    SELECT *, RANK() OVER (PARTITION BY yr ORDER BY questions DESC) AS rnk
    FROM ty
    WHERE tag IS NOT NULL
)
SELECT yr, rnk, tag, questions
FROM ranked
WHERE rnk <= 5
ORDER BY yr, rnk;

The pipe-delimited tags column is the trap: match with LIKE '%|tag|%', not equality. The CASE assigns each post to the first matching tag (say out loud that a post with two of these tags is counted once, under the first branch - a defensible simplification you should name). Then it is classic rank-within-partition. RANK vs DENSE_RANK vs ROW_NUMBER for ties is worth a sentence.

M4.2stackoverflow12 min

Question portfolio health, per year: the number of questions asked, how many got at least one answer, the answer-rate percentage, and the total score - flagging any year under 80% answered.

solution
SELECT EXTRACT(YEAR FROM creationdate)::int AS yr,
       COUNT(*) AS questions,
       COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE answercount > 0) AS answered,
       ROUND(100.0 * COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE answercount > 0)
             / COUNT(*), 1) AS answer_rate_pct,
       SUM(score) AS total_score,
       CASE WHEN 100.0 * COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE answercount > 0) / COUNT(*) < 80
            THEN 'REVIEW' END AS flag
FROM posts
WHERE posttypeid = 1
GROUP BY yr
ORDER BY yr;

The COUNT(*) FILTER pattern from Module 2, doing real work: it counts only questions clearing a threshold, in the same pass as the total. Decide and declare how you treat questions with a NULL answercount - here answercount > 0 excludes NULLs, which is the intended "no answers" behavior, but say it so nobody wonders whether you noticed.

M4.3stackoverflow13 min

Activity heatmap data: questions by day-of-week and hour-of-creation, with each cell's share of its day's total. Also name the single busiest (day, hour) cell. Mind sargability - then explain why it does not apply here.

solution
WITH cells AS (
    SELECT EXTRACT(ISODOW FROM creationdate)::int AS dow,
           EXTRACT(HOUR FROM creationdate)::int AS hr,
           COUNT(*) AS questions
    FROM posts
    WHERE posttypeid = 1
    GROUP BY 1, 2
)
SELECT dow, hr, questions,
       ROUND(100.0 * questions / SUM(questions) OVER (PARTITION BY dow), 1) AS pct_of_day
FROM cells
ORDER BY dow, hr;

-- busiest cell (self-contained: same aggregate, take the single top row):
SELECT EXTRACT(ISODOW FROM creationdate)::int AS dow,
       EXTRACT(HOUR FROM creationdate)::int AS hr,
       COUNT(*) AS questions
FROM posts
WHERE posttypeid = 1
GROUP BY 1, 2
ORDER BY questions DESC LIMIT 1;

Sargability governs predicates in WHERE - it protects index seeks. Here EXTRACT lives in SELECT/GROUP BY on a full-table aggregate; the only WHERE filter (posttypeid = 1) is a plain equality. There is no date filter for an index to serve, so the "don't wrap the column in a function" rule does not apply. Correctly scoping a performance rule impresses more than reciting it.

M4.4any10 min

Verbal finale (no SQL, timed, out loud): "Forget the tables you have been using. Design the schema you would build to track a support-ticketing system, and name the three queries leadership would run against it." Think grain, keys, indexes, and edge cases.

model answer sketch

Tables: tickets (ticket_id, customer_id FK, category, priority, opened_at, closed_at, assigned_agent_id), agents (agent_id, name, team, hire_date), and a lookup like categories. Grain statement: one row per ticket. Indexes: (assigned_agent_id, opened_at) for per-agent history, opened_at for time-window reporting, and a partial index on open tickets (closed_at IS NULL) for the live queue.

Leadership queries: (1) tickets opened and closed per month, and the backlog trend - COUNT with FILTER, grouped by month, LEFT JOIN from agents so zero-ticket agents still appear; (2) mean time-to-resolution per priority - AVG(closed_at - opened_at), and its month-over-month change via LAG, exactly the window pattern from Mock 1; (3) Pareto of ticket volume by category - percent-of-total plus cumulative percent, the P6.7 running-share trick. Close by naming edge cases: open tickets (closed_at NULL), reopened tickets (a status history table if that matters), and tickets that span month boundaries.

The move being graded is turning a vague prompt into a concrete, defensible model out loud - stating the grain first, choosing keys and indexes on purpose, and pre-empting the edge cases before the panel asks. That is exactly the reasoning every one of these mocks has been rehearsing.

After the mocks

Re-solve every missed problem from scratch the next day, not the same day. Then pull your weakest dimension from the rubric and spend one week's sessions on it: correctness misses mean re-reading the module the miss came from; fluency stalls mean re-typing (not re-reading) the pattern examples in Module 7; communication gaps mean recording yourself on two more problems. When your last two mocks both score 16+, move your daily reps to StrataScratch and DataLemur under time pressure - real interview questions, same muscles, and the final calibration before you sit the real thing.
