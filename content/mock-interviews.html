Mock Interviews

Four 45-minute simulations. These are not more practice problems - they are dress rehearsals. Set a timer, talk out loud the entire time as if an interviewer is watching, and grade yourself with the rubric afterward. Space them: one at the end of week 10, three across weeks 11-12.

The rules

  • 45 minutes hard stop. Unfinished is data - it tells you where speed work is needed.
  • Narrate everything. Restate the question, name the pattern, state the grain of your output, announce your CTE plan, call out assumptions and edge cases. Silence is the number one mock-interview failure.
  • psql or your usual client, no notes, no earlier modules open. The curriculum will not be sitting next to you in the real thing.
  • Run your queries. Sanity-check row counts and eyeball a few rows. Interviewers watch whether you verify or just declare victory.
  • Solutions stay closed until the timer ends. Then grade every question, including ones you did not reach.

Grading rubric (score each mock out of 20)

Dimension0-12-34-5
CorrectnessWrong results, unnoticedMostly right, missed an edge case (ties, NULLs, fan-out)Right, edge cases handled or explicitly called out
Pattern recognitionTrial and errorFound the pattern after a detourNamed the pattern within a minute, went straight at it
CommunicationSilent typingSome narration, gaps under pressureContinuous: plan first, assumptions stated, tradeoffs named
FluencySyntax lookups, long stallsMinor stumbles, self-correctedTyped cleanly; windows, CTEs, FILTER from muscle memory

16+ means you are ready. 12-15: re-solve every miss the next day, mock again in three days. Below 12: return to the weakest module for a week before the next mock. Log your scores - the trend across four mocks matters more than any single number.

Mock 1 - Northwind: "The new BI hire screen"

Straightforward business questions, escalating. This is what a first-round screen at most companies actually looks like.

M1.1northwind5 min

How many orders were placed in 1997, and what was the total freight cost that year?

solution
SELECT COUNT(*) AS orders_1997,
       ROUND(SUM(freight)::numeric, 2) AS total_freight
FROM orders
WHERE order_date >= '1997-01-01' AND order_date < '1998-01-01';

Half-open range, narrated: "I use a half-open range so it is index-friendly and unambiguous about timestamps."

M1.2northwind7 min

Revenue by category, highest first, with each category's share of total revenue.

solution
SELECT c.category_name,
       ROUND(SUM(d.unit_price * d.quantity * (1 - d.discount))::numeric, 0) AS revenue,
       ROUND(100.0 * SUM(d.unit_price * d.quantity * (1 - d.discount))
             / SUM(SUM(d.unit_price * d.quantity * (1 - d.discount))) OVER (), 1) AS pct
FROM order_details d
JOIN products p   ON p.product_id = d.product_id
JOIN categories c ON c.category_id = p.category_id
GROUP BY c.category_name
ORDER BY revenue DESC;

Points earned for: the discount in the revenue formula (check the schema, say you checked), and percent-of-total via window over the aggregate.

M1.3northwind8 min

Which customers have never placed an order? And separately: which customers placed orders in 1996 but none in 1998?

solution
-- never ordered: anti-join
SELECT c.customer_id, c.company_name
FROM customers c
WHERE NOT EXISTS (SELECT 1 FROM orders o WHERE o.customer_id = c.customer_id);

-- active 1996, gone by 1998: two EXISTS with opposite signs
SELECT c.customer_id, c.company_name
FROM customers c
WHERE EXISTS (SELECT 1 FROM orders o
              WHERE o.customer_id = c.customer_id
                AND o.order_date >= '1996-01-01' AND o.order_date < '1997-01-01')
  AND NOT EXISTS (SELECT 1 FROM orders o
              WHERE o.customer_id = c.customer_id
                AND o.order_date >= '1998-01-01' AND o.order_date < '1999-01-01');

Narrate why NOT EXISTS over NOT IN (NULL footgun). The second query is churn analysis in disguise - say so.

M1.4northwind10 min

Each employee's top-selling product by revenue: employee name, product name, revenue. One row per employee.

solution
WITH emp_product AS (
    SELECT o.employee_id, p.product_name,
           SUM(d.unit_price * d.quantity * (1 - d.discount)) AS revenue
    FROM orders o
    JOIN order_details d ON d.order_id = o.order_id
    JOIN products p      ON p.product_id = d.product_id
    GROUP BY o.employee_id, p.product_name
),
ranked AS (
    SELECT *,
           ROW_NUMBER() OVER (PARTITION BY employee_id
                              ORDER BY revenue DESC) AS rn
    FROM emp_product
)
SELECT e.first_name || ' ' || e.last_name AS employee,
       r.product_name,
       ROUND(r.revenue::numeric, 0) AS revenue
FROM ranked r
JOIN employees e ON e.employee_id = r.employee_id
WHERE r.rn = 1
ORDER BY revenue DESC;

Top-N per group in its most common costume. Name the pattern before typing; mention the ROW_NUMBER vs RANK tie decision.

M1.5northwind15 min

Monthly revenue for 1997 with: running YTD total, month-over-month percent change, and each month flagged 'above' or 'below' the 1997 monthly average. Present as one clean result.

solution
WITH monthly AS (
    SELECT date_trunc('month', o.order_date)::date AS month,
           SUM(d.unit_price * d.quantity * (1 - d.discount)) AS revenue
    FROM orders o
    JOIN order_details d ON d.order_id = o.order_id
    WHERE o.order_date >= '1997-01-01' AND o.order_date < '1998-01-01'
    GROUP BY 1
)
SELECT month,
       ROUND(revenue::numeric, 0) AS revenue,
       ROUND(SUM(revenue) OVER (ORDER BY month)::numeric, 0) AS ytd,
       ROUND(100.0 * (revenue - LAG(revenue) OVER (ORDER BY month))
             / NULLIF(LAG(revenue) OVER (ORDER BY month), 0), 1) AS mom_pct,
       CASE WHEN revenue > AVG(revenue) OVER () THEN 'above' ELSE 'below' END AS vs_avg
FROM monthly
ORDER BY month;

Three different windows over one aggregated CTE - running (ORDER BY), offset (LAG), and whole-set (empty OVER). If you wrote this inside 15 minutes while narrating, you passed the screen.

Mock 2 - Chinook: "The product analytics round"

M2.1chinook5 min

Top 10 customers by lifetime spend: full name, country, total, invoice count.

solution
SELECT c.first_name || ' ' || c.last_name AS customer,
       c.country,
       ROUND(SUM(i.total)::numeric, 2) AS lifetime_total,
       COUNT(*) AS invoices
FROM customer c
JOIN invoice i ON i.customer_id = c.customer_id
GROUP BY c.customer_id, customer, c.country
ORDER BY lifetime_total DESC
LIMIT 10;
M2.2chinook8 min

Which artists have tracks in the most playlists? Top 10 artists by count of distinct playlists containing at least one of their tracks. (Chain: artist, album, track, playlist_track.)

solution
SELECT ar.name AS artist,
       COUNT(DISTINCT pt.playlist_id) AS playlists
FROM artist ar
JOIN album al          ON al.artist_id = ar.artist_id
JOIN track t           ON t.album_id = al.album_id
JOIN playlist_track pt ON pt.track_id = t.track_id
GROUP BY ar.name
ORDER BY playlists DESC, artist
LIMIT 10;

DISTINCT is the whole question: an artist with 50 tracks in one playlist counts once. Say that before typing - grain awareness is what is being tested.

M2.3chinook10 min

Quarterly revenue trend with quarter-over-quarter growth, and identify the single best and worst quarter by growth.

solution
WITH quarterly AS (
    SELECT date_trunc('quarter', invoice_date)::date AS qtr,
           SUM(total) AS revenue
    FROM invoice
    GROUP BY 1
),
growth AS (
    SELECT qtr, revenue,
           ROUND(100.0 * (revenue - LAG(revenue) OVER (ORDER BY qtr))
                 / NULLIF(LAG(revenue) OVER (ORDER BY qtr), 0), 1) AS qoq_pct
    FROM quarterly
)
SELECT * FROM growth ORDER BY qtr;
-- best and worst:
SELECT * FROM growth WHERE qoq_pct IS NOT NULL
ORDER BY qoq_pct DESC LIMIT 1;
SELECT * FROM growth WHERE qoq_pct IS NOT NULL
ORDER BY qoq_pct ASC  LIMIT 1;

Excluding the NULL first quarter from best/worst is the edge case. Partial first/last quarters are worth a spoken caveat.

M2.4chinook10 min

For each country with at least 5 customers: customer count, average lifetime value, and the name of the top spender. (Two grains in one output - plan before typing.)

solution
WITH ltv AS (
    SELECT c.customer_id, c.country,
           c.first_name || ' ' || c.last_name AS customer,
           SUM(i.total) AS lifetime
    FROM customer c
    JOIN invoice i ON i.customer_id = c.customer_id
    GROUP BY c.customer_id, c.country, customer
),
ranked AS (
    SELECT *,
           ROW_NUMBER() OVER (PARTITION BY country
                              ORDER BY lifetime DESC) AS rn
    FROM ltv
)
SELECT country,
       COUNT(*) AS customers,
       ROUND(AVG(lifetime)::numeric, 2) AS avg_ltv,
       MAX(customer) FILTER (WHERE rn = 1) AS top_spender
FROM ranked
GROUP BY country
HAVING COUNT(*) >= 5
ORDER BY avg_ltv DESC;

MAX(x) FILTER (WHERE rn = 1) smuggles the row-level winner into the group-level summary in one pass - the P7.10 trick reused. Recognizing reuse across problems is exactly what fluency is.

M2.5chinook12 min

Repeat-purchase behavior: what percentage of customers made a second purchase within 90 days of their first? Narrate your definition decisions.

solution
WITH ordered AS (
    SELECT customer_id, invoice_date,
           ROW_NUMBER() OVER (PARTITION BY customer_id
                              ORDER BY invoice_date, invoice_id) AS rn
    FROM invoice
),
firsts AS (
    SELECT customer_id, invoice_date AS first_purchase
    FROM ordered WHERE rn = 1
),
seconds AS (
    SELECT customer_id, invoice_date AS second_purchase
    FROM ordered WHERE rn = 2
)
SELECT COUNT(*) AS customers,
       COUNT(s.customer_id) AS made_second_purchase,
       COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE s.second_purchase <= f.first_purchase + INTERVAL '90 days')
           AS second_within_90d,
       ROUND(100.0 * COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE s.second_purchase <= f.first_purchase + INTERVAL '90 days')
             / COUNT(*), 1) AS pct_repeat_90d
FROM firsts f
LEFT JOIN seconds s ON s.customer_id = f.customer_id;

Decisions to say out loud: denominator = all customers with any purchase; "second purchase" = second distinct invoice; LEFT JOIN keeps one-time buyers in the denominator. Definition narration is most of the grade on retention questions.

Mock 3 - Stack Overflow: "The big-data round"

Same skills, but on tables where sloppy queries visibly hurt. Runtime awareness is part of the grade here.

M3.1stackoverflow7 min

Posts per year, split into questions and answers as columns, with an answers-per-question ratio.

solution
SELECT EXTRACT(YEAR FROM creation_date)::int AS yr,
       COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE post_type_id = 1) AS questions,
       COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE post_type_id = 2) AS answers,
       ROUND(COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE post_type_id = 2)::numeric
             / NULLIF(COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE post_type_id = 1), 0), 2) AS ans_per_q
FROM posts
GROUP BY yr
ORDER BY yr;

EXTRACT in GROUP BY is fine here - no filter to keep sargable, and the whole table is being aggregated anyway. Saying that distinction earns the performance point.

M3.2stackoverflow10 min

Top 10 users by accepted answers: users whose answers were most often marked as the accepted answer on a question. (Requires the posts self-join on accepted_answer_id.)

solution
WITH accepted AS (
    SELECT a.owner_user_id, COUNT(*) AS accepted_answers
    FROM posts q
    JOIN posts a ON a.id = q.accepted_answer_id
    WHERE q.post_type_id = 1
    GROUP BY a.owner_user_id
)
SELECT u.display_name, ac.accepted_answers
FROM accepted ac
JOIN users u ON u.id = ac.owner_user_id
ORDER BY ac.accepted_answers DESC
LIMIT 10;

Aggregate the big self-join down first, join to users last - the P8.7 lesson applied without being asked. NULL owner_user_id rows (deleted users) drop out of the final join; mention it.

M3.3stackoverflow10 min

Reputation vs participation: bucket users into reputation deciles (NTILE), then per decile: user count, average posts per user, and percentage of users with zero posts.

solution
WITH post_counts AS (
    SELECT owner_user_id, COUNT(*) AS posts
    FROM posts
    WHERE owner_user_id IS NOT NULL
    GROUP BY owner_user_id
),
bucketed AS (
    SELECT u.id,
           NTILE(10) OVER (ORDER BY u.reputation) AS decile,
           COALESCE(pc.posts, 0) AS posts
    FROM users u
    LEFT JOIN post_counts pc ON pc.owner_user_id = u.id
)
SELECT decile,
       COUNT(*) AS users,
       ROUND(AVG(posts), 2) AS avg_posts,
       ROUND(100.0 * COUNT(*) FILTER (WHERE posts = 0) / COUNT(*), 1) AS pct_lurkers
FROM bucketed
GROUP BY decile
ORDER BY decile;

The LEFT JOIN + COALESCE is what keeps zero-post users in the analysis at all - an inner join would silently define lurkers out of existence and corrupt every decile. That one decision is the question.

M3.4stackoverflow15 min

Answer-speed league: for questions created in 2018, the median minutes to first answer, by month. Then: which month was fastest, and what caveat applies to December?

solution
WITH firsts AS (
    SELECT parent_id, MIN(creation_date) AS first_answer
    FROM posts
    WHERE post_type_id = 2
    GROUP BY parent_id
)
SELECT date_trunc('month', q.creation_date)::date AS month,
       COUNT(*) AS answered_questions,
       ROUND((PERCENTILE_CONT(0.5) WITHIN GROUP (
           ORDER BY EXTRACT(EPOCH FROM f.first_answer - q.creation_date) / 60
       ))::numeric, 1) AS median_minutes
FROM posts q
JOIN firsts f ON f.parent_id = q.id
WHERE q.post_type_id = 1
  AND q.creation_date >= '2018-01-01' AND q.creation_date < '2019-01-01'
GROUP BY 1
ORDER BY 1;

December caveat: right-censoring. Late-December questions have had less time to collect answers if the data snapshot ends near year-end - their unanswered ones are excluded and their answered ones skew fast. Naming censoring in a SQL interview is a differentiator few candidates reach.

Mock 4 - AdventureWorks + Taxi: "The senior panel"

Hardest set. Multi-schema joins, compound patterns, and a verbal finale.

M4.1adventureworks10 min

Sales by territory and year: territory name, year, revenue, and each territory's revenue rank within that year. Only rank 1-5 per year.

solution
WITH ty AS (
    SELECT st.name AS territory,
           EXTRACT(YEAR FROM soh.orderdate)::int AS yr,
           SUM(soh.totaldue) AS revenue
    FROM sales.salesorderheader soh
    JOIN sales.salesterritory st ON st.territoryid = soh.territoryid
    GROUP BY 1, 2
),
ranked AS (
    SELECT *, RANK() OVER (PARTITION BY yr ORDER BY revenue DESC) AS rnk
    FROM ty
)
SELECT yr, rnk, territory, ROUND(revenue::numeric, 0) AS revenue
FROM ranked
WHERE rnk <= 5
ORDER BY yr, rnk;
M4.2adventureworks12 min

Product portfolio health: per category, the number of products, the number that sold at least once, the sell-through percentage, and revenue - flagging any category under 60% sell-through.

solution
WITH product_sales AS (
    SELECT productid, SUM(linetotal) AS revenue
    FROM sales.salesorderdetail
    GROUP BY productid
)
SELECT pc.name AS category,
       COUNT(p.productid) AS products,
       COUNT(ps.productid) AS products_sold,
       ROUND(100.0 * COUNT(ps.productid) / COUNT(p.productid), 1) AS sell_through_pct,
       ROUND(COALESCE(SUM(ps.revenue), 0)::numeric, 0) AS revenue,
       CASE WHEN 100.0 * COUNT(ps.productid) / COUNT(p.productid) < 60
            THEN 'REVIEW' END AS flag
FROM production.product p
JOIN production.productsubcategory psc
  ON psc.productsubcategoryid = p.productsubcategoryid
JOIN production.productcategory pc
  ON pc.productcategoryid = psc.productcategoryid
LEFT JOIN product_sales ps ON ps.productid = p.productid
GROUP BY pc.name
ORDER BY sell_through_pct;

The COUNT(col) vs COUNT(*) LEFT JOIN behavior from Module 3, doing real work: COUNT(ps.productid) counts only matched (sold) products. Products with NULL subcategory silently drop out of the inner joins - decide and declare whether that is acceptable.

M4.3nyctaxi13 min

Demand heatmap data: trips by day-of-week and hour-of-pickup, with each cell's share of its day's total. Also name the single busiest (day, hour) cell. Mind sargability - then explain why it does not apply here.

solution
WITH cells AS (
    SELECT EXTRACT(ISODOW FROM pickup_datetime)::int AS dow,
           EXTRACT(HOUR FROM pickup_datetime)::int AS hr,
           COUNT(*) AS trips
    FROM trips
    GROUP BY 1, 2
)
SELECT dow, hr, trips,
       ROUND(100.0 * trips / SUM(trips) OVER (PARTITION BY dow), 1) AS pct_of_day
FROM cells
ORDER BY dow, hr;

-- busiest cell:
SELECT dow, hr, trips FROM cells ORDER BY trips DESC LIMIT 1;

Sargability governs predicates in WHERE - it protects index seeks. Here EXTRACT lives in SELECT/GROUP BY on a full-table aggregate; there is no filter for an index to serve, so the rewrite rule does not apply. Correctly scoping a performance rule impresses more than reciting it.

M4.4any10 min

Verbal finale (no SQL, timed, out loud): "Walk me through how you would design the tables to track our factory's robot maintenance events, and the three queries leadership would run against them." You have real domain knowledge here - use it.

model answer sketch

Tables: robots (robot_id, line, building, model, install_date), maintenance_events (event_id, robot_id FK, event_type, started_at, ended_at, technician_id, notes), maybe event_types as a lookup. Grain statement: one row per maintenance event. Indexes: (robot_id, started_at) for per-robot history, started_at for time-window reporting.

Leadership queries: (1) downtime per robot per month - SUM(ended_at - started_at) grouped by robot and month, LEFT JOIN from robots to show zero-downtime robots; (2) MTBF - gaps between consecutive failures per robot via LAG, averaged; (3) Pareto of downtime by event type - percent-of-total plus cumulative percent, exactly P6.7. Close by naming edge cases: open events (ended_at NULL), events spanning month boundaries, and planned vs unplanned as a first-class column because leadership always asks to split them.

This answer converts your MET II floor experience into data-modeling credibility - the exact bridge your resume needs to make in the room.

After the mocks

Re-solve every missed problem from scratch the next day, not the same day. Then pull your weakest dimension from the rubric and spend one week's sessions on it: correctness misses mean re-reading the module the miss came from; fluency stalls mean re-typing (not re-reading) the pattern examples in Module 7; communication gaps mean recording yourself on two more problems. When your last two mocks both score 16+, move your daily reps to StrataScratch and DataLemur under time pressure - real interview questions, same muscles, and the final calibration before you sit the real thing.
